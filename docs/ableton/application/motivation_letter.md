That experience shaped the conviction at the core of this application: the
DAW session itself — tracks, chains, routing, edits — is where production
knowledge lives, and it deserves to be a first-class object of study. To test
whether this conviction survives contact with real engineering, I built
**Session State Explorer v0**, a working prototype that represents Ableton-style
session state as a typed graph, links it to audio descriptors, and generates
recommendations that are explainable by construction — each suggestion cites
the graph nodes it reasons over and states its own limits. It also includes a
first learned baseline for the call's central goal, predicting device-chain
composition from session context. The prototype, its documentation, and a
preliminary thesis proposal accompany this application.

I am applying to the MTG specifically because the group's identity — rigorous
MIR grounded in open science and a stated commitment to trustworthy,
transparent AI for music — matches how I believe assistive production research
must be done. Essentia and Freesound are the natural substrate for the
descriptor and material layers of this work, and the chance to develop the
Cubase/VST3 instantiation of session-state graphs with Steinberg makes the
industrial half of the project concrete in a way few PhD settings could.
